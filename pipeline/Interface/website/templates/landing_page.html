Wildlife Trafficking

Illegal wildlife trade has a devastating effect on biodiversity. There is a risk of losing more than 1 million species in the coming decade. Animals like elephants, rhinos, pangolns, tigers, sharks as well as plants and trees are endangered. Criminals make billions at the expense of the lives of these animals and plants.

Illegal Wildlife Trade

Illegal wildlife trafficking is a global crisis involving the illicit trade of endangered or protected species, their parts, and products. Canada, like many countries, is impacted by this trade network. Wildlife trafficking includes poaching, smuggling, and selling animals, their parts (like ivory, horns, and pelts), and derivatives (like traditional medicines) on black markets.
It poses severe threats to biodiversity, ecosystem stability, and the survival of many species. The trade routes span continents, supplying demand for exotic pets, luxury goods, and traditional medicines, driving numerous species towards extinction. Despite international regulations and efforts to combat it, wildlife trafficking persists due to high profits, inadequate law enforcement, and demand in various markets worldwide. This illegal industry not only endangers wildlife but also fuels organized crime and poses risks to global health and security.

Severe Consequences

Rule of Law:
Wildlife crime weakens the rule of law and is linked to other serious crimes, like drug trafficking and human trafficking.
Climate:
Illegal logging and unhealthy oceans accelerate climate change.
Health:
Three-quarters of all emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic diseases, like COVID-19.
